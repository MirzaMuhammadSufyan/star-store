Power Banks Explained: Capacity, Speed, and Safety in Plain Language

mAh ratings confuse almost everyone. Here is how to pick a power bank that actually charges your phone, tablet, or laptop — without carrying dead weight.

<p>Power banks look simple: a battery in a box with USB ports. In practice, capacity labels, conversion losses, and port wattage make it easy to buy something too weak for a laptop or too heavy for a pocket.</p> <h2>Understanding mAh (and Why It Lies a Little)</h2> <p>A 10,000 mAh power bank does not deliver 10,000 mAh to your phone. Voltage conversion and heat loss reduce real-world output — often to roughly 60–70% of the printed capacity reaching your device.</p> <p>As a rough guide for phone charging:</p> <ul> <li><strong>5,000 mAh</strong> — about one partial phone charge, very portable</li> <li><strong>10,000 mAh</strong> — one to one-and-a-half full phone charges, still pocketable</li> <li><strong>20,000 mAh</strong> — multiple charges or tablet use, noticeably heavier</li> <li><strong>Above 26,000 mAh</strong> — airline restrictions may apply; check carry-on rules</li> </ul> <h2>Wattage and Port Types</h2> <p>Phones fast-charge at 18–30W on many models. Tablets want more. Laptops often need 45–65W minimum from USB-C PD.</p> <p>Read the <strong>output per port</strong>, not just total maximum. A bank advertising 65W might reserve that for a single USB-C port while USB-A stays at 12W.</p> <h2>Pass-Through Charging</h2> <p>Some power banks let you charge the bank and a device simultaneously. Convenient overnight, but often slower and warmer — which can age batteries faster. Occasional use is fine; avoid as a daily habit.</p> <h2>Airline Travel Limits</h2> <p>Many airlines limit spare lithium batteries to 100Wh (~27,000 mAh at 3.7V) without special approval. Check your airline and carry power banks in cabin baggage, not checked luggage.</p> <h2>Safety Features Worth Having</h2> <ul> <li>Over-voltage and short-circuit protection</li> <li>Temperature monitoring</li> <li>Reputable certification marks</li> <li>Clear brand with recall history you can search</li> </ul> <p>Ultra-cheap unknown brands skip protection circuitry. Saving ten dollars is not worth thermal risk near your phone and hands.</p> <h2>Charging Multiple Devices at Once</h2> <p>Splitting output across two or three ports divides the power bank&#39;s total wattage, not multiplies it. A 20W power bank charging a phone and a pair of earbuds simultaneously might deliver only 12W to the phone and 5W to the earbuds — noticeably slower than charging either device alone. Check the manual for simultaneous-use wattage figures rather than assuming each port performs at its rated maximum independently.</p> <p>This matters most for travellers charging a phone and a laptop from the same bank overnight. If the laptop needs 45W and the bank can only deliver that on a single port at a time, plan to charge devices sequentially rather than together, or budget for a higher-capacity bank with genuinely independent high-wattage ports.</p> <p>Heat is the other side effect of drawing maximum output across multiple ports simultaneously. Power banks running hot for extended periods age their internal cells faster, gradually reducing real-world capacity over the following year. If you regularly charge multiple devices overnight, a slightly larger bank run at a lower sustained load will likely outlast a smaller one pushed to its limit nightly.</p> <p>Storing a power bank at very low or very high charge for extended periods also accelerates capacity loss. If you keep one in a bag for emergencies rather than daily use, topping it up to roughly half charge every few months preserves battery health far better than leaving it fully charged or fully drained in storage.</p> <h2>Size vs Capacity Trade-off</h2> <p>Battery density improves yearly, but physics still applies. Be suspicious of tiny banks claiming laptop-level capacity at suspiciously low prices.</p> <h2>Choosing for Your Kit</h2> <ol> <li>List devices and their charging wattage</li> <li>Estimate how many full cycles you need between outlets</li> <li>Pick the smallest bank that meets wattage and cycles</li> <li>Prefer one quality bank over three cheap ones</li> </ol> <p>The right power bank disappears in your bag until you need it — then delivers exactly the power you expected.</p>
